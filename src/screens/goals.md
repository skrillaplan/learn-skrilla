# The Goals screen

Some things take years. A down payment. A car you buy outright. A cushion
thick enough to sleep on. This page holds them where you can see them,
instead of in the back of your head.

A goal has a name, an amount, and — if you want one — a target month. Then
you say what feeds it: one envelope, several, or an investment account if
your household has one.

## Two things are called goals

They're not the same thing, and it's worth thirty seconds to keep them
straight.

| | Budget-line goal | Long-term goal |
|---|---|---|
| Lives | on one line in the Budget grid | here, on its own page |
| Covers | one envelope | several envelopes, plus an account |
| Answers | "fund this much this month" | "will I have the house by 2030?" |

The long-term goal is the **why**. A budget line's *target by date* goal is
the **how** — the thing that actually moves money into the envelope each
month. Set the big one here, then fund it over there.
*→ [budget-line goals](/screens/budget/)*

## What the page tells you

For each goal: how far along you are, what you'd need to put in monthly to
hit your date, what you're **actually** putting in lately, and when that real
pace gets you there.

That last number is a projection from recent months, not a promise. It moves
when your habits move. Read it as "if the last few months keep up" — which is
still the most honest forecast anyone can give you.

Two things worth knowing before they surprise you:

- The pace is worked out from **finished** months, so a brand-new household
  sees $0/mo until one completes. Nothing is wrong; there's just nothing to
  average yet.
- A goal with nothing going in says **nothing going in yet** rather than
  inventing a date. That's the page declining to make something up.

No target month? Also fine. You get the amount and the progress, and there's
no date to be behind on. Some things are worth saving for without a countdown.

## Why your checking account isn't selectable

When you pick what funds a goal, your checking, savings, and credit accounts
are listed but grayed out: *already counted by your envelopes — pick the
envelope instead.*

Here's the reason. Your envelopes already add up to the money sitting in your
everyday accounts. Count the envelope **and** the account and you've counted
the same dollars twice — the bar fills to 60% when you're really at 30%, and
you find out the day you go to buy the thing.

Investment accounts are different, and they *are* selectable. The budget
never counted those dollars, so adding them counts them once.
