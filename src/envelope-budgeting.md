# Envelope budgeting, plainly

Before apps, people did this with paper envelopes. Rent money in one, grocery
money in another, a little in one marked "fun." When an envelope was empty,
you stopped spending — or you moved cash from another envelope, on purpose,
with your own hands.

That's the whole method. Everything else is plumbing.

## A budget is a plan for money you already have

Most budgets fail because they're forecasts. "Next month I'll make this,
so I can spend that." A forecast is a hope with a spreadsheet.

Envelope budgeting only deals in money that exists. Your paycheck lands.
Now — and only now — you decide what those actual dollars do. Not the
dollars you expect. The dollars you have.

This one rule removes most of the lying. You can't overpromise money that
isn't there, because you never budget money that isn't there.

## Give every dollar a job

Money without a job wanders. It sits in checking looking spendable, and
checking-account courage is how a fine month goes wrong.

So you assign all of it: rent gets rent, groceries get groceries, the card
payment gets its share, savings gets a named purpose instead of "whatever's
left." Whatever's left is never left — it's assigned too, even if the job
is "next month" or "fun."

This is also [why your budget isn't a bank balance](/no-bank-balances/): a
balance is money with its jobs still hidden. Envelopes are the same money
with its jobs made plain.

When there's nothing unassigned, you're done. Zero isn't broke. Zero is
*decided*.

## How a month flows

1. **Money lands.** A paycheck, a refund, a side job. It arrives jobless.
2. **You assign it.** Ten minutes, tops. Every dollar gets an envelope.
3. **You spend from envelopes.** At the store, the question isn't "do I
   have money?" — it's "does *groceries* have money?" A smaller, honester
   question.
4. **You adjust.** Reality wins somewhere every month. The car needs tires;
   dining out ran hot. Fine: move money from an envelope that can spare it.
   Moving money isn't failing the plan. Moving money **is** the plan —
   the same move your grandmother made with paper envelopes.

Then a new month starts and unspent money rolls forward. An envelope that
keeps rolling is called savings. That's all savings is: an envelope you
keep your promise to.

## Why it works when willpower doesn't

Willpower makes spending decisions at the worst possible moment — in the
store, tired, wanting the thing. Envelope budgeting moves the decision to
the best moment: payday, at a desk, calm, with the whole month in view.

You're not resisting temptation. You already decided, back when it was
easy. The envelope just remembers.

And because *joy gets an envelope too*, the plan has no forbidden fruit.
A budget with no fun in it is a diet, and diets end. The point was never
spending less on everything. The point is spending **on purpose**.

## The honest part

You will overspend an envelope. Everyone does, monthly, forever. The method
doesn't ask you to be perfect; it asks you to be honest — look at the red
number, move money to cover it, and let next month learn from it.

People who budget well aren't more disciplined than you. They just stopped
treating "the plan changed" as a moral failure and started treating it as
a Tuesday.
